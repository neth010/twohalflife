Bayesian Statistics

Bayesian statistics is an approach to data analysis the probability of where probability represents as degrees of belief in a statement, unlike a frequencies of a long run process.

In traditional statistics parameters are assumed to be fixed, but in here parameters are treated as random variables which means we can quantify the uncertainty of a parameter using probability distributions and continuously update the uncertainty as new outcome comes along.

Everything from the Bayesian statistics can represents from a single formula

P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) P(B)

Where:

  • A – parameter or hypothesis (what we want to learn about)
  • B – the observed data

There are four main pillars in this equation

The prior: P(A) ▼

The initial belief about the parameter before seen current data. It mainly based on past experiments, domain expertise or an uninformative prior.

Ex: The probability to get the "HEAD" from a flip of a fair coin is 0.5

Beta(α,β) prior — shape of your belief before seeing any data
The Likelihood: P(B|A) ▼

The probability of getting A when given the parameter B is true. It reflects how well data is modelled.

Likelihood of observed data across all possible θ values
The Posterior: P(A|B) ▼

This is the goal of the Bayesian analysis. It is the updatable probability distribution of the parameter when after take a new data into the account. It perfectly balances what knew before with what we just observed.

The marginal likelihood (evidence): P(B) ▼

This is basically a normalizing constant that ensures the probability under the posterior distribution sums for 1.

Area under the unnormalized posterior = P(B). Dividing by it makes the total area = 1.
